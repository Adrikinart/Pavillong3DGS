\subsection{Capture and pipeline configuration}
\label{sec:setup}

The subject is a carved wooden ceiling panel, stood vertical and filmed handheld in
a room with an iPhone (3840$\times$2160, 30\,fps, 196\,s). The capture is
single-sided --- the camera sweeps across the panel but never orbits it --- so
parallax between views is small, and the frames alternate between contextual
mid-range shots and extreme close-ups at grazing incidence.

Table~\ref{tab:settings} lists the configuration of the recommended model. Every
value is set in one YAML file; no code changes are required to reproduce it.

\paragraph{Resolution.}
\label{sec:res}
Per-view analysis of the 1600\,px model showed the errors were not spread evenly:
every catastrophic view (12.7--18.8\,dB) was an extreme close-up at grazing
incidence, while mid-range views reached 25.6--26.5\,dB. That is a
spatial-frequency deficit --- the pipeline was downscaling a 4K source to 1600\,px,
discarding exactly the carving detail those views demand. Re-running at 2560\,px
with denser frame sampling raised registration to 282/282 (from 181/193) and lifted
median PSNR from 23.43 to 24.48 with SSIM 0.796\,$\rightarrow$\,0.846. The aggregate
mean had concealed this entirely; only the per-view distribution exposed it.

\paragraph{Capacity.}
\label{sec:capacity}
The largest late gain came from making the model \emph{smaller}. Holding everything
else fixed and varying only \texttt{cap\_max}, PSNR and SSIM peak at 375\,k while
the catastrophic-view tail disappears below 750\,k. Cutting the budget $4\times$
from 1.5\,M gained 1.8\,dB and shrank the model $3.5\times$ (295\,MB
$\rightarrow$ 89\,MB). This is sparse-view overfitting: with low parallax the
objective is underdetermined, so surplus primitives buy configurations that fit the
training views and fail on held-out ones (\S\ref{sec:sparse}). The curve genuinely
turns rather than merely flattening --- 750\,k and 1.5\,M are both \emph{significantly}
worse than 375\,k --- so 375\,k is an optimum and not simply the smallest budget tried.
(190\,k is worse on SSIM and LPIPS but ties on PSNR; see the paired analysis below.)

The metrics disagree informatively: \textbf{LPIPS is minimised at 750\,k} and
degrades monotonically as capacity shrinks, while PSNR, SSIM and robustness prefer
less. Perceptual detail wants capacity; accuracy and generalisation want less. We
report both operating points rather than collapsing them into one recommendation.

A paired per-view test (same test views, both models) sharpens this. The
375\,k\,$\rightarrow$\,750\,k drop is real ($-0.60$\,dB, 95\,\% CI $[-1.13, -0.07]$)
and 750\,k\,$\rightarrow$\,1.5\,M more so ($-1.24$\,dB, CI $[-2.08, -0.39]$), but
190\,k\,$\rightarrow$\,375\,k is $+0.07$\,dB with CI $[-0.30, +0.43]$ --- a
\emph{tie}. So the descending arm of the curve is established, while the peak is a
plateau rather than a point: 375\,k is preferred over the PSNR-tied 190\,k on SSIM
(0.8619 vs 0.8559) and LPIPS (0.3132 vs 0.3382), not on PSNR. Reporting the mean
alone would have overstated the sharpness of the optimum.

\paragraph{Ablations and negative results.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Depth prior:} disabling it changes test PSNR by only 0.26\,dB
        (22.29\,$\rightarrow$\,22.55). It is nearly free and slightly \emph{increases}
        floater removal, but it is not what determines quality here. The
        $\approx$1.4\,dB cost of the A3 configuration is the final opacity prune, not
        the prior --- an ablation that corrected our initial assumption.
  \item \textbf{Anti-floater:} removes 18--20\,\% of primitives. Baseline models
        carried \textbf{18.2\,\%} of Gaussians below 0.02 opacity with scales up to
        1.0 (scene-spanning); the regularized model has \textbf{0\,\%} sub-0.02 haze,
        max scale 0.16, and median opacity 0.51\,$\rightarrow$\,0.88.
  \item \textbf{2DGS:} collapses to flat renders (13.3\,dB). Disk normals are
        constrained by observing a surface from multiple directions, which a
        single-sided capture does not provide.
  \item \textbf{Multi-clip merging:} SfM merges the clips cleanly (332/332 at
        0.921\,px, 266 train views) and the appearance latents demonstrably absorb
        exposure drift, yet the merged model still trails the best single-clip model
        by $\approx$1\,dB. The second clip enlarges the reconstructed volume while
        adding few views.
  \item \textbf{Generative diffusion prior:} does not help. Refining the trained
        model's held-out renders with an off-the-shelf image diffusion model (the
        training-free, post-hoc form of FixingGS) moves them monotonically
        \emph{away} from ground truth on all three metrics (PSNR 24.92 $\to$ 24.26
        $\to$ 23.54 at strengths 0.1--0.3, LPIPS worsening throughout). At 226 views and
        24.9\,dB there is no reconstruction gap for the prior to fill, only specific
        carved detail for a generic prior to corrupt; such methods target the
        extreme-sparse ($\sim$3-view) regime where the base reconstruction is broken.
  \item \textbf{Pose refinement:} costs 1\,dB. The learned deltas moved only
        $0.036^\circ$ and $7\times10^{-4}$ --- about one pixel --- because GLOMAP's
        poses were already sub-pixel accurate. Refining only training cameras (test
        poses must not be optimised) then misaligns the scene slightly relative to
        the evaluation cameras.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Densification strategy.}
Replacing the heuristic adaptive density control with 3DGS-MCMC
\cite{kheradmand2024mcmc} at the same 375\,k budget changes nothing measurable:
the paired per-view difference is $-0.09 \pm 0.45$\,dB (95\,\% CI over 28 held-out
views), i.e.\ indistinguishable. MCMC is ahead on 17 of 28 views and on median,
SSIM and LPIPS; the heuristic wins the mean only through a single $-5.32$\,dB
outlier. We therefore make no quality claim in either direction --- with 28 test
views the experiment cannot resolve differences below roughly half a decibel. The
result is nonetheless informative: it indicates the large gain in
\S\ref{sec:capacity} came from the \emph{budget}, not from how the budget is
allocated, which is what the sparse-view overfitting account predicts. MCMC remains
preferable operationally, since it eliminates the gradient threshold and makes the
budget an explicit target rather than an emergent ceiling.

\paragraph{Geometry regularisation and appearance capacity.}
Three further interventions were tested on the best configuration. \emph{(i)}
Depth--normal consistency --- the self-consistency term surface-aligned methods use
internally, applied to 3D Gaussians by compositing each primitive's least-variance
axis into a normal map and matching it against the depth-implied normal --- leaves
renders unchanged ($+0.06\pm0.21$\,dB) but produces a markedly cleaner extracted
\emph{mesh}: normal coherence rises from 0.81 to 0.92 with 32\,\% fewer vertices for
the same object. It is the right default when a mesh is the goal, and a null when only
novel views are. \emph{(ii)} A bilateral-grid appearance model, able to represent
spatially varying response, is indistinguishable from the global-affine model on the
two-clip merge ($-0.15\pm0.37$\,dB); its correction barely departs from identity
(drift 0.013 vs 0.072), because the inter-clip difference is a global exposure shift
the affine map already captures. \emph{(iii)} Completing the multi-clip capacity curve
to 375\,k gives the merge its best result (22.99\,/\,0.847), monotone with the
single-clip finding, and the per-clip breakdown shows the original clip's views
recovering to parity with the added clip --- so most of the apparent merge deficit was
budget dilution, not the merge itself.

\paragraph{Two measurement bugs worth reporting.}
Both would have silently corrupted conclusions. First, the blur filter scored
variance-of-Laplacian on the full-resolution frame, a resolution-dependent
statistic: raising the extraction resolution to 2560\,px pushed the median score
from 92 to 36 and rejected 308/400 frames as ``blurry'', which would have made the
higher-resolution run \emph{worse} than the one it replaced. Second, evaluation
rendered without restoring the appearance model, scoring held-out views on the
uncorrected output while training optimised corrected ones; fixing it moved the
multi-clip model from 19.0 to 22.0\,dB. Neither was visible in an aggregate number
--- the first surfaced from a frame-count sanity check, the second from noticing that
PSNR had collapsed while SSIM barely moved, which is the signature of a global
photometric offset rather than broken geometry.

%---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Geometry output}

Fusing the composited depth of the recommended model over all 226 training cameras
into a TSDF volume \cite{curless1996tsdf} yields a mesh of 952\,846 vertices and
1\,842\,626 triangles, cropped to the object bounding box. The panel's planks and
carved cross-members are resolved. Two limitations are inherent: volumetric
primitives give a biased expected depth, so the surface is softer than a
surface-aligned method would produce, and the fusion covers whatever the cameras
saw, so the crop is what isolates the subject from the room.

\section{Transfer to a second capture: the Casque}
\label{sec:casque}

Every result in \S\ref{sec:experiments} was obtained on one capture. A finding measured
on a single scene is not yet a method, so we reconstructed a second object chosen to be
the Pavillon's opposite on the axes that mattered, and asked which conclusions survive.

The Casque Saint-Georges is a chrome dragoon helmet with a horsehair plume, free-standing
on a checkerboard calibration target in an auditorium, captured in a full orbit with both
a professional camera and an iPhone. Against the Pavillon it inverts coverage
(orbit vs.\ single-sided), subject embedding (free-standing vs.\ carved into a wall) and
camera count. GLOMAP registers 533/536 images across four clips at 1.57\,px.

\paragraph{Masks off, deliberately.} The obvious reading of ``we only want the helmet''
is to segment it. That is the wrong move here, for two reasons we verified before
spending GPU time: \texttt{rembg}'s salient-object mask swings between 0.1\,\% and 45\,\%
of the frame across the orbit (chrome reflections, a wispy plume, a competing stand), and
the checkerboard --- which a helmet-only mask would discard --- carries essentially all
the stable SfM features, the chrome helmet having almost none. We therefore reconstruct
the whole room and isolate the helmet \emph{spatially at export}, which is possible
precisely because, unlike the wall-embedded relief, the helmet is separable in 3D.

\paragraph{Capacity inverts.} Holding everything else fixed, test PSNR rises
19.49\,$\rightarrow$\,20.35\,$\rightarrow$\,20.76 for \texttt{cap\_max} of 750\,k, 1.5\,M
and 3\,M --- the opposite direction to the Pavillon. Paired per-view over the 13 test
views, 750\,k\,$\rightarrow$\,1.5\,M is $+0.86$\,dB (95\,\% CI $[+0.34, +1.39]$, 10/13
views) and therefore real, while 1.5\,M\,$\rightarrow$\,3\,M is $+0.41$\,dB (CI
$[-0.45, +1.26]$, 8/13) and therefore a tie. The curve rises and then \emph{plateaus}
rather than turning, so 1.5\,M is the operating point: 3\,M doubles the model for a gain
inside the error bar. Transplanting the Pavillon's 375\,k would have cost over a decibel.
The transferable object is the procedure --- sweep capacity per capture, and read the
paired interval --- not the number.

\paragraph{2DGS works, once one loss weight is corrected.} Run with the default
\texttt{dist\_lambda} $=1.0$, 2DGS scores 15.79\,dB against 3DGS's 20.35, which reads as
the same failure it suffered on the Pavillon. The trajectory contradicts that reading: the
run reaches 24.6\,dB validation and is still improving at iteration 7000, which is exactly
where the distortion and normal regularizers engage, after which it decays monotonically
for the remaining 23\,k iterations. Setting \texttt{dist\_lambda} $=0$ recovers
$+4.40$\,dB (CI $[+2.32, +6.48]$, 12/13 views) and reaches 20.19\,dB --- statistically
tied with 3DGS ($-0.16$\,dB, CI $[-1.26, +0.94]$). Parity is the success condition for a
surface method: equal photometric quality while producing a surface-aligned mesh. The
mechanism is intelligible: the distortion loss concentrates ray weight onto a single
surface, which suits a bounded masked object, whereas this model must also explain a room
at widely varied depths.

The methodological point outlives the setting. The final metric alone supports ``2DGS does
not work on this capture''; the trajectory supports ``2DGS works and one weight was
misconfigured''. These prescribe opposite actions, and only the trajectory separates them.

\paragraph{Pose refinement: our explanation was wrong.} We had attributed its 1\,dB
Pavillon cost to GLOMAP's poses already being sub-pixel (0.92\,px), implying it should help
where poses are worse. The mixed pro/iPhone Casque set registers at 1.57\,px and gives
$-0.54$\,dB (CI $[-0.71, -0.36]$), improving only 9 of 53 views. Pose refinement is now
negative on both captures, so the ``poses were already too good'' account does not survive;
the likelier explanation is that our SE(3) refinement trades multi-view consistency for
per-view photometric fit. We report it as an unresolved negative rather than repair the
story after the fact.

\paragraph{What transferred unchanged.} GLOMAP, the scene-scale and learning-rate fixes,
the anti-floater bounds and prune, MCMC densification, normal-consistency and the
appearance embeddings all applied without modification and behaved as on the Pavillon. The
explicit object box remains a negative on both: concentrating the budget on the helmet
produced no sharper helmet and added boundary smearing, because the helmet is
data-limited rather than budget-limited.

\paragraph{What the Gaussian budget is actually sizing.}
The 16$\times$ gap between the two captures' optima (375\,k versus 6\,M) invites the reading
that parallax sets the budget. It does not. Re-running the identical pipeline with the
photometric loss restricted to the helmet --- same poses, same split, masks applied only to
the loss --- flattens the capacity curve completely:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrrrr}
\toprule
\texttt{cap\_max} & 190\,k & 375\,k & 750\,k & 1.5\,M & 6\,M \\
\midrule
test PSNR (loss masked to the subject) & 22.81 & 23.10 & 23.03 & 22.96 & 23.06 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Every adjacent pair is a statistical tie (widest paired delta $+0.29$\,dB, 95\,\%
CI $[-0.13, +0.72]$), across a 32$\times$ range. The same pipeline scoring the whole frame
gains significantly at \emph{every} doubling up to 6\,M.}
\label{tab:masked}
\end{table}

So the transferable statement is not that capacity inverts between captures, but that
\textbf{the budget is set by how much observed scene the loss must reproduce}: one wall
$\rightarrow$ 375\,k, an auditorium seen across 310$^\circ$ $\rightarrow$ 6\,M, the helmet
alone $\rightarrow$ $\leq$190\,k --- below the Pavillon's optimum, on the capture with far
\emph{more} parallax. For an object deliverable, masking the loss yields a 30$\times$
smaller model (190\,k versus 5.3\,M primitives, ${\approx}$50\,MB versus 3.8\,GB) at no
measurable cost. Absolute PSNR in Table~\ref{tab:masked} is not comparable to the unmasked
figures, since the pixel population differs; only the shape of the curve is.

\paragraph{Validation must be measured on the subject.}
The same asymmetry corrupts model selection. Casque validation PSNR peaks near iteration
7000, declines for 20\,000 steps, then jumps 4.4\,dB at the final iteration --- while the
helmet region rises monotonically throughout (22.20 $\rightarrow$ 24.71\,dB). The decline
belongs entirely to the background, which stays unsettled until the MCMC noise scale decays;
the stored renders show the subject stable from early on while the room flips between dark
and correct. Because \texttt{best\_val} and the early-stopping check both read the
whole-frame number, a patience of three would have terminated a healthy run near iteration
10\,000. The trainer now scores a subject-only pass whenever masks are available and selects
on it, logging both metrics so the discrepancy remains visible.

Two plausible explanations were eliminated by measurement before this one was found: the
periodic floater opacity-suppression firing in the same iteration as validation (the rule
suppresses zero Gaussians on this model), and accumulated near-transparent Gaussians fogging
the render (pruning everything below 0.005 opacity mid-training moves PSNR by 0.00\,dB).
Both were plausible, and both were wrong; the rendered images settled it.
